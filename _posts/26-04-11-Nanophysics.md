# 🌐 Nanoscience & Nanotechnology – Introduction

<div style="border-left:6px solid #6f42c1; padding:12px; background:#f8f5ff;">
The most fascinating and rapidly advancing field in modern <b>Basic Sciences and Engineering</b> is <b>Nanotechnology</b>, which has revolutionized scientific and technological development.
</div>

---

## 🔍 What is “Nano”?

<div style="background:#e6fffa; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #2c7a7b;">
The word <b>nano</b> comes from a Greek word meaning <i>“dwarf”</i> or extremely small.
</div>

$$
1 \text{ nm} = 10^{-9} \text{ m} = 10^{-3} \mu m = 10 \, \text{Å}
$$

👉 A human hair thickness ≈ **50,000 nm**

---

## 🧪 Nanoscience vs Nanotechnology

<div style="border:1px solid #d0d7de; padding:12px; border-radius:8px; background:#f6f8fa;">
<b>Nanoscience:</b> Study of phenomena at atomic and molecular scale where properties differ significantly.<br><br>
<b>Nanotechnology:</b> Design, manipulation, and application of materials at nanometer scale.
</div>

---

## 🧠 Key Concepts

- Study of **materials at atomic & molecular scale**
- Control of **shape, size, and properties**
- Applications in **science, engineering, medicine, and industry**

# 🧱 Nanomaterials

## 📌 Definition

<div style="background:#f0fff4; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #38a169;">
Nanomaterials are materials having at least one dimension less than <b>100 nm</b>.
</div>

---

# 🧩 Classification of Nanomaterials

---

## I. 🌍 Based on Origin

### 🔹 Natural Nanomaterials
- Viruses  
- Protein molecules  
- Clay minerals  
- Milk, blood (colloids)  
- Fog (aerosols)  
- Shells, corals, bones  
- Spider silk, lotus leaf, gecko feet  

---

### 🔹 Artificial Nanomaterials
- Carbon nanotubes  
- Quantum dots  
- Semiconductor nanoparticles  

---

## II. 📐 Based on Dimensions

<div style="background:#fffaf0; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #d69e2e;">
Classification based on number of nanoscale dimensions
</div>

### 🔸 0-D (Zero Dimensional)
- All dimensions in nanoscale  
- Example: Gold nanoparticles, Quantum dots  
- Size: **1–50 nm**

---

### 🔸 1-D (One Dimensional)
- One dimension outside nanoscale  
- Example: Nanowires, Nanorods, Nanotubes  

---

### 🔸 2-D (Two Dimensional)
- Two dimensions outside nanoscale  
- Example: Thin films, Nano sheets  

---

### 🔸 3-D (Three Dimensional)
- All dimensions outside nanoscale  
- Example: Bulk nanomaterials, nanocomposites  

---

## III. 🧬 Based on Structural Configuration

### 🔹 Carbon-Based Nanomaterials
- Fullerenes (spherical)  
- Carbon nanotubes (cylindrical)  

---

### 🔹 Metal-Based Nanomaterials
- Gold nanoparticles  
- Silver nanoparticles  
- Titanium dioxide  

---

### 🔹 Dendrimers
- Highly branched macromolecules  

---

### 🔹 Composites
- Colloids  
- Gels  
- Copolymers  

---

# ⚙️ Synthesis of Nanomaterials

<div style="border-left:6px solid #0366d6; padding:12px; background:#f1f8ff;">
Nanomaterials are synthesized using two major approaches:
<b>Top-Down</b> and <b>Bottom-Up</b>
</div>

---

## 🔻 Top-Down Approach

<div style="background:#fff5f5; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #e53e3e;">
Breaking bulk material into nanoscale particles
</div>

### ✔ Features
- Large-scale production possible  
- No chemical purification required  
- Expensive  
- Poor control over size  
- Impurities may be introduced  

### 🔬 Examples
- Ball milling  
- Electrochemical etching  
- Photolithography  

---

## 🔺 Bottom-Up Approach

<div style="background:#f0fff4; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #38a169;">
Building material atom-by-atom or molecule-by-molecule
</div>

### ✔ Features
- Better control over size and structure  
- Requires chemical purification  
- Less expensive  
- Produces ultra-fine particles  
- Fewer impurities  

### 🔬 Examples
- Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)  
- Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD)  
- Sol-Gel method  

---

## ⚖️ Comparison

| Feature | Top-Down | Bottom-Up |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Cost | High | Lower |
| Control | Poor | Good |
| Purity | Less | High |
| Scale | Large-scale | Limited |

---

# ⚖️ Comparison of Properties: Nanomaterials vs Bulk Materials

<div style="border-left:6px solid #d63384; padding:12px; background:#fff0f6;">
Nanomaterials exhibit <b>unique optical, electrical, magnetic, and mechanical properties</b> that are significantly different from their bulk counterparts due to their nanoscale dimensions.
</div>

---

# 🎨 Key Property Differences

---

## 🌈 1. Colour

<div style="background:#f0fff4; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #38a169;">
Nanomaterials show different colours compared to bulk materials.
</div>

- Bulk gold → **Yellow**  
- Nano gold → **Red**

📌 Reason:
- Gold nanoparticles absorb **blue-green light (~450 nm)**  
- Reflect **red light (~700 nm)** → appear red  

---

## 🔥 2. Melting Point

<div style="background:#fff5f5; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #e53e3e;">
Melting point decreases significantly at nanoscale.
</div>

- Nanoparticles melt at **lower temperatures** than bulk  
- Observed in:
  - Nanowires  
  - Nanotubes  
  - Nanoparticles  

📌 Reason:
- Large **surface-to-volume ratio**
- Altered thermodynamic properties  

---

## 💪 3. Mechanical Strength

<div style="background:#ebf8ff; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #3182ce;">
Nanomaterials exhibit higher mechanical strength.
</div>

- Strength can be **1–2 times higher** than bulk materials  
- Due to:
  - Fewer defects  
  - Improved crystal structure  

📌 Applications:
- Cutting tools (high hardness & durability):
  - Tungsten carbide  
  - Tantalum carbide  
  - Titanium carbide  

---

## ⚡ 4. Electrical Properties

<div style="background:#faf5ff; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #6b46c1;">
Electrical resistivity increases at nanoscale.
</div>

- Increased **surface scattering**
- Reduced particle size → modified electronic structure  

📌 Key Concept:
- When size ≈ **electron de Broglie wavelength**
  - Band structure becomes discrete  
  - Band gap increases  
  - Resistivity increases  

---

## 👁️ 5. Optical Properties

<div style="background:#fffaf0; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #d69e2e;">
Optical properties like colour and transparency change significantly.
</div>

### Examples:

| Material | Bulk Property | Nano Property |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| Gold | Yellow | Red |
| Silicon | Grey | Red |
| Zinc Oxide | White (scatters light) | Transparent |

📌 Reason:
- Particle size < wavelength of visible light  
- Reduced light scattering  

---

## 🧲 6. Magnetic Properties

<div style="background:#f0fff4; padding:10px; border-left:5px solid #38a169;">
Nanoparticles exhibit <b>superparamagnetism</b>.
</div>

### 🔹 Features:
- Not magnetic without external field  
- Quickly magnetized when field applied  
- Lose magnetism when field removed  

### 🔹 Materials:
- Magnetite (Fe₃O₄)  
- Maghemite (Fe₂O₃)  

### 🔹 Applications:
- Drug delivery  
- MRI contrast agents  
- Bioseparation  
- Medical diagnostics  

---

# 🔍 Why Properties Differ?

---

## 📌 1. Large Surface Area to Volume Ratio

<div style="border-left:6px solid #0366d6; padding:12px; background:#f1f8ff;">
Nanomaterials have extremely high surface area compared to bulk materials.
</div>

### ✔ Effects:
- Increased chemical reactivity  
- Faster reactions  

# 📊 Surface Area to Volume Ratio (Cube Example)

<div style="border-left:6px solid #6f42c1; padding:12px; background:#f8f5ff;">
Demonstration of how <b>Surface Area to Volume (SA/V) ratio increases</b> as cube size decreases.
</div>



## 📐 Formulae for Cube

$$
\text{Surface Area} = 6a^2
$$

$$
\text{Volume} = a^3
$$

$$
\frac{SA}{V} = \frac{6a^2}{a^3} = \frac{6}{a}
$$

---

# 📦 Stepwise Reduction of Cube Size

---

## 🔶 Case 1: Side = 10 mm

<div style="background:#fffaf0; padding:12px; border-left:5px solid #d69e2e;">

$$
\text{Surface Area} = 6 \times (10)^2 = 600 \, \text{mm}^2
$$

$$
\text{Volume} = (10)^3 = 1000 \, \text{mm}^3
$$

$$
SA/V = \frac{600}{1000} = 0.6 \, \text{mm}^{-1}
$$

</div>

---

## 🔷 Case 2: Side = 5 mm

<div style="background:#ebf8ff; padding:12px; border-left:5px solid #3182ce;">

$$
\text{Surface Area} = 6 \times (5)^2 = 150 \, \text{mm}^2
$$

$$
\text{Volume} = (5)^3 = 125 \, \text{mm}^3
$$

$$
SA/V = \frac{150}{125} = 1.2 \, \text{mm}^{-1}
$$

</div>

---

## 🟢 Case 3: Side = 2 mm

<div style="background:#f0fff4; padding:12px; border-left:5px solid #38a169;">

$$
\text{Surface Area} = 6 \times (2)^2 = 24 \, \text{mm}^2
$$

$$
\text{Volume} = (2)^3 = 8 \, \text{mm}^3
$$

$$
SA/V = \frac{24}{8} = 3 \, \text{mm}^{-1}
$$

</div>

---

## 🟣 Case 4: Side = 1 mm

<div style="background:#faf5ff; padding:12px; border-left:5px solid #6b46c1;">

$$
SA = 6 \times (1)^2 = 6 \, \text{mm}^2
$$

$$
V = (1)^3 = 1 \, \text{mm}^3
$$

$$
SA/V = \frac{6}{1} = 6 \, \text{mm}^{-1}
$$

</div>

---

# 📊 Summary Table

| Side (mm) | Surface Area (mm²) | Volume (mm³) | SA/V (mm⁻¹) |
|----------|------------------|-------------|------------|
| 10 | 600 | 1000 | 0.6 |
| 5  | 150 | 125  | 1.2 |
| 2  | 24  | 8    | 3   |
| 1  | 6   | 1    | 6   |

---

# 📈 Key Observation

<div style="border-left:6px solid #28a745; padding:12px; background:#f0fff4;">
As the size of the cube decreases, the <b>Surface Area to Volume ratio increases dramatically</b>.
</div>

---

## 🔁 Mathematical Insight

$$
\frac{SA}{V} = \frac{6}{a}
$$

👉 As $$a \downarrow$$,  
$$
\frac{SA}{V} \uparrow
$$

---

# 🚀 Conclusion

- Smaller particles → **higher surface area**
- Higher SA/V → **greater reactivity**
- This is the key reason behind **unique properties of nanomaterials**

---

## ⚛️ 2. Quantum Confinement Effect

<div style="background:#fff5f5; padding:12px; border-left:5px solid #c53030;">
Electrons are confined in very small space → changes energy levels.
</div>

### 🔹 Explanation:

- Bulk material → continuous energy bands  
- Nanomaterial → discrete energy levels  

### 🔹 Result:

$$
\text{Band gap increases}
$$

- Higher energy gap → restricted electron motion  
- Alters:
  - Optical properties  
  - Electrical properties  
  - Magnetic properties
